# Why hospitals settle

The hardest judge question isn't technical. It's "why would a hospital ever agree to this?" The answer is economics, not goodwill: the alternative to settling is worse for the hospital, on their own numbers.

## 1. The collections alternative is bad math

| Path | Gross recovery | Timeline to first payment | Agency fee |
|---|---|---|---|
| Send to collections | 15-25% of balance (one industry survey: 15.3%) | 45-60+ days | 15-40% contingency, higher on older debt |

Net that out and the hospital keeps roughly 20% of the balance or less, months after discharge. In-house cost-to-collect runs ~2% of net collections under HFMA's own best-practice benchmark (3-4%+ for smaller systems), so a same-day cash settlement at 25-50% of balance beats an agency referral outright, before the agency even takes its cut.

## 2. Their own posted price is the anchor

CMS's price-transparency rule standardized the machine-readable file format on July 1, 2024, with penalties running past $2M/year for noncompliance. Every hospital already posts a discounted cash price per code, whether they advertise it or not.

Our demo hospital's real posted cash price for the code set, pulled from MGH's actual published file, is **$2,633.25**, already below the **$4,287** balance on an **$8,432** bill. Commercial insurers' negotiated median, **$999.30**, is below even the cash price. The number we're citing on the call is the hospital's own.

## 3. The compliance clock (nonprofits, IRC 501(r))

- No Extraordinary Collection Actions for 120 days from the first post-discharge statement.
- 30-day written notice required before any ECA.
- 240-day window to apply for financial assistance.

Settling inside that window keeps the hospital off the clock entirely.

## 4. Who can actually say yes

A front-line rep can write off a few dollars on their own judgment. Anything real needs a supervisor, and supervisors need a documented reason code to sign off. A cited duplicate CPT, a coding review, the hospital's own posted price: that's the paper trail that lets a supervisor approve instead of stalling.

## 5. This is standard practice, not a special favor

Prompt-pay discounts of 20-40% for paying in full today are normal industry practice. Uninsured cash rates already run 30-70% below chargemaster. The ask sits inside the range hospitals already operate in.

## 6. Credit reporting, stated carefully

The CFPB rule banning medical debt from credit reports was vacated in federal court in July 2025, so it isn't law. What actually holds are the credit bureaus' own voluntary policies: paid medical collections get removed, balances under $500 aren't reported, and there's roughly a 12-month grace period before anything appears at all.

## The demo, in these terms

Maya's balance is $4,287. The settlement closes at $1,650 same day: 38.5 cents on the dollar, today. Route the same balance to an agency instead, and even a clean recovery nets the hospital something like 20 cents on the dollar, arriving six months or more out, after a 15-40% contingency cut. Same-day cash at nearly double the eventual agency payout, with no aging and no contingency fee, is the rational move for the hospital.
