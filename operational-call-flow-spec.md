# The Negotiator — Operational Call-Flow Spec for a Medical-Bill Voice Agent

## TL;DR
- **Who to call and in what order is the single biggest structural decision:** the highest-yield path is provider-side (hospital "Patient Financial Services"/billing → financial-assistance/charity-care office → billing supervisor/financial counselor with write-off authority), because AJMC's peer-reviewed survey found patients succeed **63% of the time negotiating with providers vs. 37% with insurers**; separate professional-service bills (radiology, anesthesiology, ER physician group) must each be called individually.
- **The mechanics that measurably move outcomes are: get the itemized bill/UB-04 first, ask the non-confrontational opener "Is this negotiable?", name the specific levers (prompt-pay discount, financial-assistance/charity-care threshold, self-pay rate), be persistent across multiple calls, and always close by capturing a reference number + rep name + written confirmation.** Survey data shows most people who actually negotiate get a reduction (AKASA/YouGov: 78%; LendingTree: 93%) — the hard part is doing it and persisting.
- **For voice design, the evidence supports a warm-but-competent voice at ~150 wpm (0.9–1.1x), lower pitch for authority on numbers, ElevenLabs Flash v2.5 for sub-500ms latency, and mirroring the rep's pace**; the AI-disclosure penalty is real (Luo et al. 2019: disclosure cut purchases by more than 79.7%) but must be balanced against FCC/TCPA disclosure rules — disclose briefly and early, then win on competence and empathy.

## Key Findings

### 1. The map of who to call
- **Provider beats payer.** The one hard, peer-reviewed number on target selection: in Kyanko & Busch (*American Journal of Managed Care*, 2016; 2011 internet survey, n=721 privately insured adults, 1,081 out-of-network bills), patients "were more likely to be successful negotiating with providers compared with insurers (**63% vs 37%**; P<.01)." Start provider-side for discounts/charity care; only go to the insurer for coding/coverage/EOB errors and denials.
- **Departments are named differently by every system but map to four functions:** (1) main **billing office / "Patient Financial Services" / "Patient Account Services"** (Mayo Clinic uses "Patient Account Services," Sutter Health uses "Patient Financial Services"); (2) **financial-assistance / charity-care office** (often a *different phone number* than billing — Triage Health explicitly warns "This number may be different from the hospital's billing office"); (3) **self-pay/uninsured desk**; (4) **third-party collections agency** once the debt is sold.
- **Separately-billed units:** hospital-based outpatient care generates a hospital facility fee AND a physician professional fee (Hackensack Meridian Health documents this explicitly); radiology, anesthesiology, pathology, ER physician groups, and assisting surgeons (TEFRA-mandated) bill separately and must each be contacted. One "medical event" can require 3–6 separate negotiation calls to different entities.
- **Who actually has authority:** front-line reps usually cannot approve write-offs. Escalate to **billing supervisor, patient financial counselor/navigator, patient advocate, or revenue cycle manager.** Consumer Reports quotes Health Advocate's Abbie Leibowitz: "The first response will be no, I can't do anything. You have to be persistent. Figure out who in the organization has the authority." Keep asking for the manager "even if that takes you all the way up to the chief financial officer."
- **How to find the number:** on the bill (account/guarantor number + billing phone), on the EOB (insurer's member-services line), and via the hospital website — searching "[hospital name] financial assistance" or "[hospital name] 501(r) financial assistance policy" (both CMS and Dollar For recommend this exact search).

### 2. IVR / call-routing obstacles and tactics
- **Reality:** A Gartner/Software Advice audit of 50 Fortune 500 IVRs found most forced callers through **three menu levels before offering a live-agent ("zero-out") option**, and fewer than a quarter offered it in the first menu.
- **Tactics (GetHuman/AARP/Seattle Times consumer guidance):** press 0 (or 0#, #0, *0) repeatedly; say "agent"/"representative"/"get human"; stay silent/mumble to trigger "invalid entry" routing to a human; select the *sales* or *Spanish* option (answered fastest) then ask for transfer; a viral-but-tested trick is entering "0# 0# 0#" (Daily Mail found it worked at 5 of 8 major companies tested). For a voice agent, pre-map the IVR path and use stored DTMF tone sequences (pause/wait characters).
- **"Your call may be recorded":** this is standard; the agent should assume it is being recorded and behave accordingly.

### 3. Turn-by-turn call scripts (from advocates and documented cases)
- **Opener + self-ID:** "Hi, my name is [X]. I recently received a bill and I'd like to discuss it with someone in billing." (MMI, Dollar For.)
- **The "three magic words":** Marshall Allen (ProPublica) documents patient "Laurie" who asked simply **"Is this negotiable?"** — the rep replied "I can give you a 50% discount," saving her $825 on a $1,650 balance. Allen's analysis: it's non-confrontational and "there's only one truthful answer... 'Yes.'"
- **Request the itemized bill first (My Beautiful Budget verbatim script):** "Hi, I'd like to request a fully itemized bill for account number [X]. Please send it to me in writing with every CPT code listed. I'm not paying anything until I've reviewed it." This buys ~30 days and signals sophistication.
- **UB-04 upgrade (Goodbill):** for outpatient visits, ask for the **UB-04** not just an "itemized bill," because "there's only one definition of a UB04" — hospitals can't equivocate. For inpatient, request both.
- **Walk the flags one at a time (My Beautiful Budget):** "A few line items I'd like to discuss" is the polite version of "I'm disputing charges." For each: ask the rep to explain, then state what your research shows. "The billing rep didn't write the charge. They have an internal 'adjustment' button and you want them to use it on you."
- **Name the levers explicitly (they will not be volunteered):** "What is your prompt-pay discount?" (typically 10–25% off); "What is your financial-assistance threshold?" (usually 200–400% of the federal poverty line); ask for the self-pay/uninsured rate.
- **Settlement offer (Dollar For / Inspired Budget verbatim):** "I see the balance is $___, but I can pay you $___ today and settle the bill as a $0 balance." Inspired Budget's author called ~10 places and only 2 refused; cut 25–40% off most bills.
- **Collections script (Dollar For):** first ask diagnostic questions — "Is interest accruing?" / "Will you sue me?" / "Did you buy the debt or does the hospital still own it?" / "Is there a predetermined cash-settlement amount?" — then: "I want to offer a settlement of $___ to be paid in full to close out this account. I've worked very hard to save this money. This is all I can afford. Will you accept?" Marshall Allen notes third parties buy debt for **2–5 cents on the dollar** and may accept ~15%.
- **Hardship framing (Community Health Advocates):** state employment status, household size, financial obligations, and that "pursuing me for these bills will force me and my family into further financial hardship."
- **Never leave a voicemail:** Allen's collaborator calls it "a felony" to leave a message — it forfeits the power position; keep calling until you reach a live person.

### 4. What measurably increases success
- **Just asking works — but few people ask.** AKASA/YouGov survey of 2,026 U.S. adults (Mar 9–14, 2022): "Sixty-four percent reported never having challenged the validity or accuracy of a medical bill," and of the 36% who did, "**78 percent successfully had the disputed charges reduced or removed**." LendingTree/Qualtrics survey of 1,550 Americans (Feb 19–22, 2021): "**93%** who negotiates a medical bill has their bill reduced or dropped altogether." The binding constraint is initiation + persistence, not tactic sophistication.
- **Persistence / multiple calls:** Vox/KFF documents Martha Shea reducing a $12,000 surgery bill to $7,800 over **six months and many calls**, getting incremental knock-downs each time ("each time I called, the physician group would knock it down a little bit"). Goodbill reports one $3,900 reduction took **26 phone calls** to finalize. KFF's blanket advice: "Persistence is key — it may take several calls."
- **Itemized bill in hand:** advocates uniformly treat obtaining the itemized bill/UB-04 as prerequisite. The often-cited ~80% error rate is most directly attributable to Becker's Hospital Review citing Medical Billing Advocates of America ("approximately 80% of medical bills are reported to contain errors"); broader industry analyses report a 49–80% range, and hospital bills over $10,000 average ~$1,300 in errors.
- **Charity-care assistance dramatically raises approval:** Dollar For's "Path to Charity Care" report — "only **29%** of patients with hospital bills they cannot afford are able to learn about, apply for, and receive charity care," and per the 2023 survey "**51%** who were potentially eligible for assistance did not apply," most citing not knowing about it; approval reaches **67% when aided by an organization or hospital staff.**
- **Gender / who negotiates:** AJMC found men more successful than women (76% vs 50%) in OON negotiation; the broader meta-analysis (Mazei et al. 2015, *Psychological Bulletin*) confirms women get worse economic-negotiation outcomes on average — but the gap **narrows or reverses when advocating on behalf of someone else** (Harvard/HKS research). This is relevant: an agent negotiating *for* a patient sits in the advantaged "other-advocacy" frame.
- **Must-use phrases (advocate consensus):** "Is this negotiable?", "financial hardship," "charity care" / "financial assistance," "prompt-pay discount," "self-pay rate," "settle as paid in full," and requesting a "supervisor," "patient financial counselor," or "patient advocate" by title.
- **Resolution takes time:** AKASA — 27% took 1–3 months, 18% took 3–6 months, 16% took more than 6 months. Multi-call sequencing is the norm, not the exception.

### 5. Voice / vocal delivery (the part specific to a voice agent)
- **Speaking rate:** converging evidence points to **~150–210 wpm as the persuasive sweet spot.** Miller et al. (1976, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*) found fast speakers (195 wpm) rated more credible/persuasive than slow (102 wpm). University of Michigan (Benki et al.) found interviewers speaking **~3.5 words/sec (~210 wpm)** got the most agreement — faster or slower did worse — and that **~4–5 short pauses/minute** beat perfectly fluent delivery (too fluent "sounds too scripted"). National Center for Voice and Speech pegs average US English at ~150 wpm; practitioner consensus for calls is 140–160 wpm.
- **Pitch:** peer-reviewed evidence trades off — **lower pitch reads as more competent, dominant, and authoritative** (Oleszkiewicz et al. 2016; Scientific Reports 2022 found dominance "strongly and negatively related to voice pitch"), while **higher pitch reads as warmer** and, in some financial-exchange contexts, more trusted. The Touro University sales-pitch study found **high-pitched voices had a negative impact on buyer interest.** Design implication: lower/steady pitch when stating numbers and firm positions; slightly warmer when building rapport.
- **Vocal tone (Wang, Lu, Li, Khamitov & Bendle, "Audio Mining," 2021, 10,721 Kickstarter pitches):** successful persuasion associates with tones denoting **(1) focus, (2) low stress, (3) stable emotions** — which listeners read as competence.
- **Warmth vs. competence (*Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science*, 2026, 28,000+ real insurance-company calls):** **warmth wins for low-severity issues; competence wins for high-severity issues, where "warmth can backfire"** because customers prioritize resolution. A large bill dispute is "high-severity," so the agent should lead with competence/decisiveness and use warmth as seasoning. Broader finding (Güntürkün et al. 2020): competence drives transactional outcomes (the discount), warmth drives relational ones.
- **Accent:** bias exists but is conditional — "You Lost Me at Hello" (*Journal of Retailing*/ScienceDirect) found accent bias surfaces mainly **when the service outcome is unfavorable**; Zendesk (2011) found CSAT dropped from 79% to 58% when call centers moved offshore. Design implication: a neutral/standard-American or region-matched accent minimizes downside risk.
- **Gender:** consumers "generally prefer female and extroverted VA voices" (arXiv literature review), and female voices are often perceived as warmer; but for authority on numbers the pitch effect matters more than gender per se.
- **"Smile" and confident/"debating" tone raise trust** (Torre et al.; Dubiel et al., via arXiv 2405.04791).

### 6. AI-voice-specific design (ElevenLabs)
- **Model:** Use **ElevenLabs Flash v2.5** (~75ms generation latency) for the live call — ElevenLabs explicitly advises against v3 for real-time agents due to higher first-token latency. Reserve v3 (with expressive audio tags and ~68% fewer errors on numbers/formulas) only for pre-rendered voicemail drops.
- **Settings (ElevenLabs docs):** Speed **0.9–1.1x** ("most natural conversations"); **Stability ~0.5–0.6** (balances dynamism vs. consistency); tune Similarity for clarity. Recommended stock voices for a professional agent: **Stuart** ("professional & friendly"), **Archer** ("grounded and friendly… with charm"), **Alexandra**, or **Angela** ("great listener… down to earth"). Voice Design can generate a region-matched custom voice (age/accent/tone/pacing).
- **Latency budget:** target **≤500ms median turn latency**, hard ceiling ~800ms, never exceed ~1,000–1,500ms (hang-up zone). Human baseline inter-turn gap is ~200ms (Stivers et al., *PNAS* 2009). Monitor **P95/P99 tail latency**, not just median. Use a **smart end-of-turn detector** (silence + prosody + lexical completeness) starting ~500ms silence, tightened to ~250–350ms; **bias toward waiting** (false interruptions feel worse than slight delay, especially while a rep reads account numbers) and support **barge-in**.
- **Mirroring:** dynamically match the rep's words-per-minute using STT metadata.
- **AI disclosure:** Luo et al. (2019, *Marketing Science*, 38(6):937–947; randomized field experiment, 6,255 outbound sales calls) — undisclosed chatbots were "as effective as proficient workers and four times more effective than inexperienced workers," but "a disclosure of chatbot identity before the machine–customer conversation reduces purchase rates by **more than 79.7%**"; a **late-disclosure strategy** and prior AI experience mitigate this. Ishowo-Oloko et al. (2019, *Nature Machine Intelligence*) established the transparency–efficiency tradeoff. **However**, this measured consumer purchases, not trained-rep cooperation, and the **FCC's 2024 TCPA ruling** treats AI voices as "artificial/prerecorded," with proposed rules requiring disclosure at call start. Net recommendation: disclose briefly and clearly early (company + purpose + AI status, per law), then rely on competent, empathetic delivery; A/B-test disclosure timing where legally permitted.

### 7. Call mechanics & documentation
- **Always capture, before hanging up:** call reference/confirmation number, rep name and/or ID, the specific agreed action, and a promised resolution date. Practitioner guidance (PracticeSolutions): "never end a phone call with an insurance company without a reference number." Repeat it back for accuracy (RequestLetters).
- **Get it in writing:** NFCC's McClary (via AARP): "Don't take their word over the phone, an informal handshake, a high-five. None of that is worth anything." Follow every call with a recap email: "Per our call today, please confirm [outcome] by [date]."
- **A "structured outcome" at call end = one of:** (a) discount/write-off approved with confirmation number + written follow-up; (b) charity-care/financial-assistance application initiated with reference + deadline; (c) payment plan agreed (get the policy in writing, ideally 0% interest); (d) documented decline — rep name, date, reason, reference number — which becomes the basis for escalation (supervisor, written dispute via certified mail, state regulator, CFPB at (855) 411-2372, or No Surprises Act complaint at 1-800-985-3059).
- **Multi-call sequencing (recommended architecture):** Call 1 = information gathering (request itemized bill/UB-04, confirm department, get financial-assistance policy, note who has authority); Call 2 = negotiation (levers + settlement offer); Call 3 = confirmation (verify written agreement, reference numbers). Closing everything in one call is realistic only for simple prompt-pay discounts, not larger disputes given documented multi-month timelines.

## Details

**Source-quality notes.** The strongest evidence in this brief: AJMC's provider-vs-insurer success split (peer-reviewed survey), the speech-rate and pitch/warmth–competence literature (peer-reviewed), Luo et al. and Ishowo-Oloko et al. on AI disclosure (peer-reviewed field/lab), and Stivers et al. on turn-taking (PNAS). Medium-strength: LendingTree/AKASA/Dollar For surveys (self-reported, but large samples) and the 28,000-call warmth/competence field study. Weakest and explicitly flagged: vendor-blog conversion percentages tying specific voice traits to close rates (e.g., "matched accent +35%," "enthusiasm −40%") — these have no disclosed methodology and should be treated as A/B-testable hypotheses, not facts. Individual patient anecdotes (Laurie, Martha Shea, the 26-call Goodbill case) are illustrative single cases, not representative statistics.

**On the provider-side advantage.** The 63%/37% split is the most decision-relevant single statistic for call routing. It reflects that providers have discretionary write-off authority and chargemaster prices that are pure fiction, whereas insurers are bound by plan contracts and adjudication rules. The corollary: use the insurer call narrowly — to fix coding errors, confirm in-network processing, appeal denials, and get a reference number for the EOB — then take the corrected balance back to the provider to negotiate.

**On voice, reconciling the pitch conflict.** The literature genuinely conflicts: dominance/competence favors lower pitch; some financial-trust contexts favor higher. The operational resolution is prosodic *variation within a call* — lower, falling-intonation delivery on numbers, firm positions, and the settlement ask (signals authority/certainty); warmer, slightly higher delivery during rapport and hardship framing. This also escapes the monotone uncanny valley.

## Recommendations

1. **Build the call flow provider-first.** Default target sequence: hospital Patient Financial Services → financial-assistance/charity-care office → escalate to billing supervisor/financial counselor. Route to the insurer only for coding/coverage/denial issues. Detect separately-billed professional services (radiology, anesthesia, ER physician, pathology) and spawn a distinct call per entity.
2. **Enforce a three-call state machine** (gather → negotiate → confirm) with persistence logic: if declined, escalate to a named authority and schedule a callback rather than accepting the first "no." Benchmark to change course: if 3 escalations within a system all decline and no charity-care path exists, switch to written certified-mail dispute + regulator/CFPB complaint.
3. **Hard-code the lever checklist into the negotiation turn:** "Is this negotiable?" → itemized-bill/UB-04 verification → prompt-pay discount → financial-assistance threshold → self-pay rate → settlement offer → payment-plan fallback.
4. **Voice-design starting spec:** ElevenLabs Flash v2.5; a warm-but-competent professional stock or region-matched custom voice; ~150 wpm with 4–5 short pauses/min; lower/steady pitch on numbers; Stability ~0.5–0.6, Speed 0.9–1.1x; ≤500ms median latency with smart endpointing (~300–500ms) and barge-in; mirror the rep's pace.
5. **Disclosure:** implement a brief, early, compliant AI + company + purpose identification to satisfy FCC/TCPA/state law, then optimize delivery for competence and empathy. Where legally permitted, A/B-test disclosure phrasing/timing against reference-number-confirmed outcomes.
6. **Documentation is a first-class feature, not an afterthought:** every call must end by extracting and storing reference number, rep name/ID, agreed action, and callback date, and auto-send a recap email. A "documented decline" is a valid, structured terminal state that triggers the escalation branch.
7. **Instrument outcomes** (reduction %, calls-to-resolution, which lever closed it, disclosure variant, voice variant) so the agent's own call data becomes the A/B evidence base the literature can't provide.

## Caveats
- Most quantitative success rates (LendingTree 93%, AKASA 78%) are **self-reported survey data with selection bias** — people who negotiate and succeed may over-report. The AJMC 63/37 figures are from a **2011 survey**, pre-No Surprises Act, and about out-of-network bills specifically.
- The AI-disclosure penalty (>79.7%) is from a **B2C sales context**; its magnitude for a trained billing/insurance rep is unknown and could be smaller. There is countervailing peer-reviewed evidence that **non-disclosure carries ethical/reputational and now legal risk**, and that counterparts who know they're dealing with AI may **bargain harder or less honestly** ("ethical fading," *Journal of Business Ethics* 2025).
- Vendor-sourced voice-conversion percentages are **unverified marketing** and are flagged as such throughout.
- The legal landscape is **in flux**: FCC AI-voice rules were proposed (Aug 2024) and state laws vary; credit-reporting rules for medical debt shifted (the CFPB's 2025 rule was vacated by courts, but the bureaus' voluntary $500/one-year policies remain). Confirm current law before deployment.
- Department names, phone routing, and authority thresholds **vary by hospital system**; the agent needs per-system configuration or robust real-time discovery ("May I ask who has authority to approve an adjustment?").
