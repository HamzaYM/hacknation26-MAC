The Negotiator

A medical bill-lowering voice agent — the US billing system end-to-end, rendered as workflows, decision logic, and reference panels. Every threshold, statistic, and caveat from the source brief is carried here verbatim.
01

Overview — Why This Matters

The Problem
Medical pricing is opaque. Chargemaster list prices are largely fictional — per Bai & Anderson (Health Affairs, June 2015), hospital charges averaged 3.4× the Medicare-allowable cost in 2012 (mode 2.4×; the highest-markup facility, North Okaloosa Medical Center, FL, charged 12.6× cost; 49 of the top 50 were for-profit). The EOB ("not a bill", from the insurer) and the itemized bill (from the provider) frequently don't match — and that gap is where most leverage lives.
The Opportunity
Insurers negotiate list prices down; uninsured/self-pay patients are exposed to the full inflated number — which is precisely why self-pay discounts of 40–60% exist. Denials are common: per KFF (March 2026), HealthCare.gov insurers denied 19% of in-network and 37% of out-of-network claims in 2024 (~20% combined; 3%–36% range across insurers; roughly 85 million in-network claims denied).
Core Thesis
A three-lever negotiation stack, applied in sequence: (1) error/coding disputes → (2) statutory rights → (3) price benchmarking. Combining all three routinely yields 30–70% reductions on out-of-pocket balances; charity care alone can eliminate 50–100%. The Caller routes dynamically: insurer first when the EOB is wrong; provider first when the balance is unaffordable or the bill contains errors.
Schema mandate: key every config to CPT/HCPCS codes and DRGs; ingest the itemized bill AND the EOB as separate objects; store a per-line fair price band (a multiple of Medicare, e.g. 150–250%). The gap between the billed/chargemaster amount and this band is the negotiator's quantified ask.

Sequential chaining: insurer-first and provider-first commonly chain — fix the insurer's adjudication first (which may erase or shrink the balance), then negotiate the residual with the provider. Collectors cannot fix coding errors or grant charity care — those go back to the provider.
Who has authority to reduce a bill: uninsured/self-pay discounts routinely 40–60% off chargemaster (e.g., Med Center Health: 20% self-pay discount + an additional 30% for attested uninsured patients, and 60% at one of its facilities) · financial assistance/charity care (required of nonprofit hospitals under §501(r); many cover up to 300–400% of the Federal Poverty Level) · prompt-pay/lump-sum discounts (commonly 10–30%, sometimes higher) · error disputes · No Surprises Act balance-billing disputes. Front-line reps have limited authority — always ask for a billing supervisor, financial counselor, or patient advocate/ombudsman.

Appendix — Caveats & Sourcing (Verbatim)

  • The "80% of bills contain errors" statistic is an advocacy-industry estimate (Medical Billing Advocates of America), not a peer-reviewed or government figure, and reflects a self-selected sample of bills patients already suspected — present it as a range (≈49–80%) and attribute it. Use it for product motivation, not as a hard claim in patient-facing reports.
  • On dispute success rates: the best primary evidence (Duffy et al., JAMA Health Forum, Aug 2024) found that ~74% of people who reached out about a suspected billing mistake had it corrected and ~62% who negotiated got a price drop — but these rest on small subsamples (37 and 14 respondents respectively), so treat them as directionally encouraging, not precise.
  • Discount percentages are highly variable by hospital, state, income, and whether payment is immediate. The ranges here are planning defaults, not guarantees; make every percentage a config parameter.
  • Voice-agent authorization is a legal/consent issue. To speak with an insurer or provider on a patient's behalf, the agent needs a HIPAA-compliant authorization on file; build consent capture into the Estimator.
  • State law frequently overrides the federal floor (e.g., ground-ambulance balance billing, California's Hospital Fair Pricing Act, stronger charity-care mandates in CA/IL/NY/MA). The config must be state-aware.
  • CMS transparency-file data quality is uneven and the CY2026 dollar-amount requirements were still phasing in with enforcement beginning April 2026; treat MRF values as corroborating, not sole, evidence.
  • Some cited percentages (self-pay discounts, settlement ranges) come from advocacy blogs and vendor pages; the primary-source anchors (RAND 254%, Bai/Anderson 3.4×, KFF denial rates, CMS $400/120-day, IRS §501(r) AGB, the 2023 credit-bureau changes) are the ones safe to hard-code.
Safe to hard-code
RAND 254% of Medicare (2022) · Bai/Anderson 3.4× cost markup (12.6× outlier) · KFF denial rates (19% in-network / 37% OON, 2024) · CMS $400 GFE threshold / 120-day window / $25 fee · IRS §501(r) AGB & 240-day FAP window · 2023 credit-bureau changes (<$500 removed, paid collections removed, 1-year delay).
Directional / advocacy estimates
Error-rate range (≈49–80%) · dispute-success rates (~74% corrected, ~62% price drop; JAMA Health Forum small subsamples of 37 and 14) · discount percentages (self-pay 40–60%, prompt-pay 10–30%, settlements 25–50¢ on the dollar, combined 50–86%).
